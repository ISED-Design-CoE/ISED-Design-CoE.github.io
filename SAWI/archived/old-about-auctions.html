About spectrum auctions

ISED manages spectrum to maximize economic and social benefits for Canadians. When the demand for spectrum is not expected to exceed the supply, ISED generally uses a first come, first served licensing process to award spectrum licences. When the demand for spectrum is expected to exceed supply, a competitive licensing process, such as an auction, is generally used.

How does a spectrum auction work?

Through a structured process involving stakeholder consultation, competitive bidding, and regulatory oversight, these auctions enable efficient distribution of limited spectrum to service providers while maximizing public benefit and fostering innovation in wireless communications.

The following is a summary of the auction process as described in the Framework for spectrum auctions in Canada.

1: Consultation

The spectrum auction consultation process involves publishing a notice in the Canada Gazette, collecting initial stakeholder comments over 45-90 days, and potentially allowing a second reply period where all feedback is publicly posted online.

2: Decision, framework and key dates

Following the consultation periods, the Minister of Industry publishes final policy decisions in the Canada Gazette, detailing licenses and rules, while allowing for clarification questions and amendments that are published online as official supplements to the policy.

3: Application

To participate in the auction, applicants must submit their applications following the application requirements with ownership details and deposits, after which ISED publishes lists of all applicants and later qualified bidders with their eligibility information. ISED will communicate with qualified bidders with information needed for the auction period.

4: Auction period

The auction process begins on predetermined dates specified in the table of key dates, while the conclusion depends on the auction design and format rather than following a fixed schedule.

5: Post auction

Following the auction's close, provisional winners must submit documentation and make full payment for winning bids and penalties before receiving their licenses. Any unsold or forfeited licenses may be offered later through residual auctions or alternative processes such as first-come, first-served.

Auction types

Auctions

Auctions are also called major auctions, internet-based auctions or band auctions. Learn more about auctions and its process.

Residual auctions

Residual auctions are auctions of licences that remain unallocated or have been returned. Learn more about residual auctions and its process.

Auction formats

Sealed-bid auction

Format where bidders submit sealed offers simultaneously; the highest bid wins, ensuring confidentiality and preventing influence from competitors.

Combinatorial (package) bidding

Allows bidders to place bids on combinations of licences, allowing more flexible, efficient assignment of spectrum.

Second-price rule

Awards the licence to the highest bidder, but they pay the amount of the second-highest bid instead.
