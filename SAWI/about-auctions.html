About spectrum auctions

Innovation, Science and Economic Development Canada (ISED) manages spectrum to maximize economic and social benefits for Canadians. Auctions are a competitive licensing process generally used to award spectrum licences when the demand for spectrum is expected to exceed supply.

How does a spectrum auction work?

Through a structured process involving stakeholder consultation, competitive bidding, and regulatory oversight, these auctions enable efficient distribution of limited spectrum to service providers while maximizing public benefit and fostering innovation in wireless communications.

The following is a summary of the auction process as described in the Framework for spectrum auctions in Canada.

Auctions

Auctions release or reallocate significant amounts of spectrum for commercial use, typically across the country. Before an auction begins, a public consultation process takes place to determine its attributes and design, such as the exact frequency ranges to be auctioned, the number and size of frequency blocks, reserve prices, and measures to support competition. Once an auction begins, it can take several weeks of bidding before it concludes. Spectrum auctions follow the approach described in the Framework for Spectrum Auctions in Canada and generally take place according to the steps below.

1. Consultations on the proposed policy and licensing procedures

A formal consultation paper is published proposing all aspects of the upcoming auction. This document outlines the proposed spectrum policy, auction design, and licensing procedures and rules. The consultation process provides interested stakeholders with the opportunity to review and comment on these proposals before the auction framework is finalized.

2. Comment period

During this time, interested parties can submit written comments on the proposed auction design and rules. All stakeholders including potential bidders, industry associations and public interest groups may participate. After the closing date for receipt of comments, copies of all the comments received are posted on ISED’s website.

3. Reply comment period

During this time, stakeholders can respond to comments submitted during the initial comment period. This provides an opportunity for addressing concerns or refining proposals based on feedback. After the closing date for receipt of reply comments, copies of all the reply comments received are posted on ISED’s website.

4. Publication of the licensing framework/decision

After all submitted feedback has been reviewed, the final auction framework is published. This document discusses the comments received from the consultation process and describes the auction format, bidding rules and licence conditions. A table of key dates associated with the auction accompanies this document.

5. Clarification questions

Stakeholders have an opportunity to submit written questions asking for clarification of rules or policies related to the auction. Questions received by the deadline and ISED’s written answers to these questions are published online.

6. Deadline for the receipt of applications

Prospective participants must submit their completed application forms, supporting documentation and financial deposit by the published deadline. The specific application requirements are detailed in the auction framework.

7. Publication of the list of applicants

Shortly after the application submission deadline, ISED publishes online a comprehensive list of all applicants, as well as information about their beneficial ownership, affiliations and associations. This step does not imply that participants have been qualified to participate in the auction.

8. Publication of the list of qualified bidders

ISED publishes the official list of qualified bidders on its website. After this time, bidders receive training materials and a schedule for the first week of the auction.

9. Bidder information sessions and mock auctions

Qualified bidders receive training and become familiar with the auction rules, software platform and bidding procedures. Bidders participate in practice sessions on the actual auction software so they may identify and resolve any technical issues before the start of the auction.

10. Start of auction

The auction starts on the date specified in the auction’s table of key dates.

11. Conclusion of auction

The duration and conclusion of an auction depend on its design and format as well as bidding activity. Details on the closure of an auction are provided in the framework.

12. Publication of provisional licence winners

Shortly after the conclusion of the auction, the provisional results, including provisional winning bidders, prices and their licences are published online. Results are not considered final until all payments have been received.

13. Initial payment deadline

Provisional winners must submit their first payment by the published deadline. Failure to do so may result in forfeiture of licences won.

14. Final payment deadline

Provisional winners must submit their final payment (balance owing) by the published deadline. Failure to do so may result in forfeiture of licences won.

15. Issuance of licences

Once provisional winning bidders have submitted all payments on the final payment deadline, their licences are issued. At this time, all auction results are deemed final.

16. Publication of bidding data

A few months after issuing licences, ISED publishes on its website detailed bidding data, which includes bidders' identities and all bids submitted for the concluded auction. This is done in the interest of transparency of the auction process.

Residual auctions

Residual auctions are auctions of licences that remain unallocated at the conclusion of an auction, or of previously assigned licences that have been returned to ISED. Residual auctions follow the general approach described in the Streamlined Framework for Auctioning Residual Spectrum Licences, and generally do not require a consultation process.

The process for residual auctions by sealed bid generally includes the following steps:

1. Publication of notice/decision

2. Deadline for the receipt of applications

Submission period when prospective participants file their completed application forms with required documentation, including beneficial ownership information and financial deposits. The specific application requirements are detailed in the auction's consultation documents and the published licensing framework.

3. Publication of the list of applicants, beneficial ownership and affiliate associate information

Shortly after the application submission deadline, ISED publishes a comprehensive list of all applicants on its website. This disclosure includes information about beneficial ownership and affiliations between applicants, but does not indicate qualification status for auction participation.

4. Publication of the list of qualified bidders

ISED publishes the official list of qualified bidders on its website, specifying the licenses each bidder is eligible to bid on and their initial eligibility points for the auction.

5. Start of auction (sealed bid deadline)

6. Conclusion of auction

7. Publication of provisional licence winners

Official announcement of auction results, including disclosure of winning bidders, licenses won, and final prices, with a summary of the auction process.

8. Initial payment deadline

Date by which winners must submit their first payment installment, typically a percentage of the total winning bid amount. Failure to meet this deadline may result in forfeiture of winning bids.

9. Final payment deadline

Due date for payment of the balance of the winning bids, which must be payed before licenses can be issued.

10. Issuance of licences

Formal granting of licenses to winning bidders who have fulfilled all payment obligations. This marks the conclusion of the auction process.

Auction formats

The following links bring you to the framework for residual auctions.

Sealed-bid auction

Format where bidders submit sealed offers simultaneously; the highest bid wins, ensuring confidentiality and preventing influence from competitors.

Combinatorial (package) bidding

Allows bidders to place bids on combinations of licences, allowing more flexible, efficient assignment of spectrum.

Second-price rule

Awards the licence to the highest bidder, but they pay the amount of the second-highest bid instead.
