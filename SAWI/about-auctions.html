The following is a summary of the auction process as described in the Framework for spectrum auctions in Canada.

Auctions release or reallocate significant amounts of spectrum for commercial use, typically across the country. Before an auction begins, a public consultation process takes place to determine its attributes and design, such s the exact frequency ranges to be auctioned, the number and size of frequency blocks, reserve prices, and measures to support competition. Once an auction begins, it can take several weeks of bidding before it concludes. Spectrum auctions follow the approach described in the Framework for Spectrum Auctions in Canada and generally take place according to the steps below.

Residual auctions are auctions of licences that remain unallocated at the conclusion of an auction, or of previously assigned licences that have been returned to ISED. Residual auctions follow the general approach described in the Streamlined Framework for Auctioning Residual Spectrum Licences, and generally do not require a consultation process.

The process for residual auctions by sealed bid generally includes the steps below.

The clock auction format is a bidding process that includes two stages: a clock stage and an assignment stage.

The clock stage determines the number of generic blocks that a bidder will win and the price for these blocks. The assignment stage determines the specific frequencies that will be assigned to each winning bidder and the additional price for the specific frequencies.

As an example, this format is detailed in section 10 and annexes D and E of the Consultation on a Policy and Licensing Framework for Spectrum in the 3800 MHz Band.

The combinatorial clock auction format is a bidding process that allows bidders to bid on packages of licences instead of individual licences, eliminating the risk that bidders may win some but not all of the licences that they desire.

The CCA includes a clock stage and an assignment stage. In the clock stage, there is a supplementary round allowing bidders the flexibility to submit additional packages of interest that they were unable to express during the clock rounds. All bids during the clock and supplementary rounds are binding and are used in determining the winners and the prices to be paid.

As an example, this format is detailed in section 7 and annex C of the Technical, Policy and Licensing Framework for Spectrum in the 600 MHz Band.

With this bidding process, specific licences (which include service areas and frequencies) are offered simultaneously in a single stage. Bidding is organized into a series of rounds, and information on the bids placed by all bidders is made available after each round. ISED sets the bid increments and bidders simply opt in or out of bidding.

As an example, this format is detailed in section 6 and appendix B of the Licensing Framework for the Auction for Spectrum Licences for Advanced Wireless Services and other Spectrum in the 2 GHz Range.

This format is generally used for residual auctions. In a sealed-bid auction, bidders simultaneously submit sealed bids on the licence(s) they are interested in so that no bidder knows the bids of any other participant.

As an example, this format is detailed in section 8 of the 2024 Notice of Upcoming Auction of Residual Spectrum Licences.
