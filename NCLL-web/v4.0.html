Non-competitive local licensing (NCLL)

Easy access to spectrum

Overview

Innovation, Science and Economic Development Canada (ISED) regulates and oversees the regulation and licensing of radio frequency spectrum throughout the country. More information about spectrum, and our role in managing it, can be found at Spectrum in Canada.

ISED has introduced a framework known as non-competitive local licensing (NCLL). This framework is designed to promote the growth of innovative business models, encourage continuous investment in cutting-edge wireless technology like 5G networks, and enhance connectivity in Canada.

Benefits

 

Simple licence application process

Provides a simple, automated and easy-to-understand licensing application process.

Created for communities, businesses and industry

Offers small communities, businesses, industry verticals (e.g. campuses, factories or farms), and small wireless service providers a solution to meet their unique needs.

Designed for success

Facilitates the coexistence between NCLL operations through ISED's automated licensing system.

Affordable licence fees

Offers affordable licence fees to encourage innovative solutions.

Use cases

Wireless Internet

Deliver wireless Internet services in areas where traditional wired connections are not available or feasible, such as rural or remote locations.

Smart factories

Improve efficiency and productivity by connecting industrial Internet of Things (IoT) devices, enabling real-time monitoring and control of machinery and equipment.

Healthcare facilities

Improve medical device connectivity for real-time monitoring of healthcare devices and support telemedicine services in remote consultations with healthcare professionals.

 
Port and train operations

Optimize cargo handling processes and increase throughput by tracking container, crane automation, and vehicle management.

Educational campuses

Implement campus-wide connectivity, remote learning support, and immersive educational experiences using augmented reality (AR) and virtual reality (VR) technologies.

Smart agriculture

Optimize resources and increase yields by deploying wireless networks for precision farming applications, including crop monitoring, irrigation control, and livestock management.

 
Mining and extraction

Improve productivity and worker safety in challenging environments by establishing wireless networks to enable remote monitoring of equipment, autonomous vehicles, and safety systems.

Fixed wireless access (FWA)

Deliver wireless connectivity to homes and small businesses.

Enhanced Mobile Broadband

Delivers high-speed data and video streaming in both indoor and outdoor settings, offering effective solution for enhancing wireless services in crowded areas like stadiums, airports, and venues.

About NCLL

The NCL licensing framework has several key features.

On this page

  • Managed spectrum
  • First come, first served
  • Customized licence areas
  • Flexible licence terms
  • Deployment requirements
  • No leasing or trading
  • NCLL spectrum browser
  • Licence fees

Managed spectrum

ISED performs technical analyses to facilitate coexistence between NCLL operations and other services.

First come, first served

Completed NCLL applications are reviewed on a first come, first served basis.

Customized licence areas

A licensee defines a licence area (up to 12 coordinate points anywhere in Canada) during the application process. Whether it's a single building, a large mine or farm, or an entire remote community, you tell us what area you want to cover.

Flexible licence terms

Licences can be renewable or temporary.

Renewable licences can be renewed annually as long as the  licence conditions (3900 MHz band / 28 GHz band) are met.

Temporary licences are available for periods between 1 day and 11 months.

Deployment requirements

ISED aims to ensure that spectrum is actually used, and is not warehoused or resold. Any spectrum that is not used will be made available to others.

As a licensee, you have two years to deploy at least one station and begin operating your network.

You will need to demonstrate to ISED that you're covering your licence area on an ongoing basis by uploading your site data. You must start uploading site data within one month of receiving your licence, and continue doing so on a monthly basis.

No leasing or trading

A non-competitive local (NCL) licence is only to be used by the licensee. It is not transferable in whole or in part and cannot be leased, traded, sub-divided or subordinated in frequencies or geographic areas.

NCLL spectrum browser

The NCLL spectrum browser is an online tool that allows you to search for existing NCL licences and other radio services in your desired areas. This spectrum browser can be a valuable tool for planning and can also generate your coordinates in WKT format, which can be easily copied directly into the licence application form.

Licence fees

NCLL has affordable licence fees that encourage innovative use and applications.

The annual licence fee is calculated by the following equation:

Annual licence fee = Base rate for area type × total bandwidth (MHz) × area size (km²)

Note that all fees listed in this section, including the calculator, are as of 2024. Fees are subject to be adjusted annually in accordance with the Consumer Price Index (CPI). Please see Spectrum and telecommunications fees for current licence fees for NCLL.

Base rate comparison table
Area type Base rates, 3900 MHz band Base rates, 28 GHz band
Metro and urban $1.80 $0.18
Rural $0.45 $0.045
Remote $0.01 $0.001

There is a minimum annual fee of $48.00 for all NCL licences.

For renewable licences authorized during the course of a fiscal year, or for non-renewable short-term licences authorized for durations of less than one year, ISED will apply a prorated fee of 1/12th of the total annual fee for each calendar month (in whole or in part). The minimum monthly fee is $4.00.

Example fee scenarios

Example 1: Yearly fee for a university campus

John has an NCL licence for a 10 km² university campus in downtown Vancouver for 20 MHz of spectrum.

John's yearly fee:

$1.80 (Urban base rate) × 20 MHz × 10 km² = $360.00

Example 2: Yearly fee for a remote area in Saskatchewan

Beth has an NCL licence to provide wireless service to rural communities north of Saskatchewan. She's using 20 MHz of spectrum and the licence area covers 85 km².

Beth's yearly fee is:

$0.45 (Rural base rate) × 20 MHz × 85 km² = $765.00

Example 3: Fee for a month-long event downtown Toronto

Jordan has an NCL licence to boost wireless connectivity for a month-long event with thousands expected to attend in a 0.54 km² area in downtown Toronto.

Jordan's fee for one month is:

($1.80 (Urban base rate) × 20 MHz × 0.54 km²) / 12 months = $1.62

Because of the minimum fee of $4.00 per month, Jordan's total fee for one month is $4.00.

Example 4: Fee for a two month outdoor event in Ottawa

Sarah has an NCL licence to boost wireless connectivity for two months for a racing event that covers 12 km² of Ottawa.

Sarah's fee for two months is:

($1.80 (Urban base rate) × 20 MHz × 12 km²) / 12 months × 2 months = $72.00

Bands

NCLL is currently available in the following bands:

Band Frequency range Band plan Maximum bandwidth Area size limits
3900 MHz 3900 MHz to 3980 MHz Eight blocks of 10 MHz each 20 MHz
  • Low power: up to 15 km²
  • Medium power: 75 to 165 km², not allowed in urban areas
28 GHz 27.50 GHz to 28.35 GHz Seventeen blocks of 50 MHz each 400 MHz
  • Low power: up to 1 km²
  • High power: 5 to 50 km²

3900 MHz band

The 3900 MHz band, 3900–3980 MHz, lies within the mid-band frequencies. It comes with decent propagation characteristics and is well suited for high-speed applications. This makes it ideal for rural broadband as well as a solid choice to support 5G wireless connectivity for applications including resource extraction and agriculture.

Band plan

Note that commercial mobile wireless services may be operating in the neighbouring 3800 MHz band in the vicinity of NCL licensees. Any NCLL operations on block A1 (3900-3910 MHz) won't be protected from commercial mobile wireless operations, nor may they impact them.

The upper portion of the band is reserved for smaller operators. Any entity, or affiliate of that entity (as defined in section 6.2 of SPB-001-23), with 100,000 or more retail mobile wireless phone subscribers is ineligible to hold a licence for the blocks from 3940 MHz to 3980 MHz.

Bandwidth

NCL licences in the 3900 MHz band have two options for bandwidth:

  • 10 MHz
  • 20 MHz (i.e. two contiguous 10 MHz blocks)

For example:

  • if a user is assigned one block, such as Block A2: 3910-3920 MHz, they will have 10 MHz of bandwidth
  • if a user is assigned two contiguous blocks, such as Blocks A5 and A6: 3940-3960 MHz, they will have 20 MHz of contiguous bandwidth

It is also possible to apply for two non-adjacent 10 MHz blocks, in two separate applications. For example, a user can apply separately for Block A3 (3920-3930 MHz) and Block A7 (3960-3970 MHz).

In general, NCL licensees in the 3900 MHz band may not have a total of more than 20 MHz in any location. However, under certain circumstances it is possible to request that this limit be waived. Please see SAB-03-25 for details.

Licence types

The 3900 MHz band offers two licence types, low power and medium power. A low power licence might be ideal for industrial and enterprise applications, particularly for localized or indoor activities. On the other hand, a medium power licence could be more appropriate to service larger outdoor areas.

Power specifications comparison table
Low power Medium power
Max permissible EIRP 20 dBm/MHz 37 dBm/MHz
Area size 0 to 15 km² 75 to 165 km²
Area type(s) Metro, urban, remote and rural Only remote and rural
Max outdoor antenna height 10 metres 50 metres
Max indoor antenna height No limits No limits

The area types correspond to one of four categories within the Tier 5 service areas.  For more information, see Service areas for competitive licensing or see the NCLL spectrum browser found on the NCLL services page.

Licensees can establish a mix of indoor and outdoor stations for both low power and medium power licence types. SRSP-521 specifies the technical requirements for NCLL in the 3900 MHz band. The maximum equivalent isotropically radiated power (EIRP) indicates the system total transmit output power effectively radiated by the transmission antenna in a specific direction. Licensees intending to operate exclusively indoors need a low power licence type. Indoor operations have EIRP that is limited to 1 watt per 10-MHz frequency block, or 2 watts per 20-MHz block.

In metro and urban areas, the EIRP is limited to 1 watt per 10-MHz frequency block, or 2 watts per 20-MHz, with an antenna height limit of 10 m.

In rural and remote areas there can be one of the following two options for EIRP and antenna height:

  1. same technical limits as for metro and urban areas
  2. EIRP of 50 watts per 10 MHz frequency block, or 100 watts for 20 MHz, with antenna height limit of 50 m

You can apply for multiple licences to cover larger areas.

28 GHz band

The 28 GHz band, 27.5–28.35 GHz, lies within the high-band millimetre-wave (“mmWave”) frequencies. It is has very large bandwidth capacity over very short distances, allowing for improved delivery of high resolution data. This allows operators to provide high-density connectivity for real-time communication in factories or to provide connectivity to large gatherings in stadiums.

Band plan

Note that commercial mobile wireless services may be operating in the neighbouring upper 26 GHz band in the vicinity of NCL licensees. Any NCLL operations on block AA1/AA2 (27.50-27.60 GHz) won't be protected from commercial mobile wireless operations, nor may they impact them.

The upper portion of the band is reserved for smaller operators. Any entity, or affiliate of that entity (as defined in section 6.2 of SPB-001-23), with 100,000 or more retail mobile wireless phone subscribers is ineligible to hold a licence for the blocks from 27.90 GHz to 28.35 GHz.

Bandwidth

NCL licences in the 28 GHz band can include from one to eight 50-MHz blocks (50 to 400 MHz). NCL licensees in the 28 GHz band may not have a total of more than 400 MHz in any location.

Licence types

The 28 GHz band offers two licence types, low power and high power. A low power licence might be ideal for industrial and enterprise applications, particularly for localized or indoor activities. On the other hand, a high power licence could be more appropriate to fixed wireless access services located in outdoor areas.

Power specifications comparison table
Low power High power
Max permissible EIRP 52 dBm/100MHz 75 dBm/100MHz
Area size 0 to 1 km² 5 to 50 km²
Area type(s) Metro, urban, remote and rural Metro, urban, remote and rural
Max outdoor antenna height 15 metres 50 metres
Max indoor antenna height No limits No limits

The area types correspond to one of four categories within the Tier 5 service areas.  For more information, see Service areas for competitive licensing or see the NCLL spectrum browser found on the NCLL services page.

Licensees can establish a mix of indoor and outdoor stations for both low power and high power licence types. SRSP-522 specifies the technical requirements for NCLL in the 28 GHz band. For outdoor operations there can be one of the following two options for EIRP and antenna height:

  1. Low power: EIRP of 52 dBm/100MHz, with antenna height limit of 15 m
  2. High power: EIRP of 75 dBm/100MHz, with antenna height limit of 50 m

You can apply for multiple licences to cover larger areas.

FAQ

General

Is 3900 MHz a 5G band?

Yes, the 3900 MHz band is a 5G band.

The 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP) has identified a 5G frequency band that covers the 3900-3980 MHz (i.e. n77), resulting in access to an equipment ecosystems that could support different types of networks or use cases. Following this, in May 2021, ISED published the Decision on the Technical and Policy Framework for the 3650-4200 MHz Band and Changes to the Frequency Allocation of the 3500-3650 MHz Band. In this policy framework, ISED designated the 3900-3980 MHz band for shared usage to leverage the emerging equipment ecosystems for 5G technology and provide opportunities for users including wireless Internet service providers (WISPs), utilities, and industry verticals.

Note that licensees can deploy 5G or other wireless technologies as long as ISED's technical standards are met (i.e. SRSP-521, Technical Requirements for Non-Competitive Local Licensed Services, including Fixed and/or Mobile Systems, and Flexible Use Broadband Systems, in the Band 3900-3980 MHz, RSS-198, Flexible Use Broadband Equipment Operating in the Band 3900-3980 MHz).

Is there a list of current licensees?

Yes, there is a list of current licensees.

This information can be found at Spectrum Management System. You can also find out other licensees in your area using the NCLL spectrum browser found on the NCLL services page.

Where do I find information about site upload requirements?

ISED is adopting a simple demonstration of deployment (a "yes/no" system) to meet the condition of licence on deployment. Details on how to upload information will be found on the SMS site data upload website. Alternatively, you will also be able to find information in this video.

How can I create multiple adjacent licence areas?

You can create multiple adjacent licence areas by performing the following steps in the NCLL portal:

1. Duplicate the application/licence that you wish to create an adjacent area licence for by clicking "Duplicate" under "Actions" in the portal.

2. Edit the licence area of the newly created duplicate application:

  • you can edit the licence area either by modifying the coordinates listed in the "Boundary points" table, or by re-drawing the area using the drawing tool
What are my important licence deadlines?

There are two important deadlines you should keep in mind as a licensee. They are as follows:

  • you have to upload licence site data every 30 days
  • you have to deploy at least one station within 2 years of your licence's effective date

Fees and invoices

How are the licence fees calculated?

A licence fee calculator is available to determine the total licence fee.

The annual licence fee for a non-competitive local licence is calculated by multiplying the base rate for the area type selected (metro and urban; rural or remote) multiplied by the total frequency blocks in MHz, multiplied by the area size in km².

How are fees calculated if an area overlaps two different area types?

If any portion of an NCL licence falls within a remote area, the fees are calculated using the remote base rate. Otherwise, if any portion of an NCL licence is located in a metro/urban area, then fees are calculated using the metro/urban base rate.

This table below shows which area type is used to determine the applicable base rate in case of overlap. Commonly referred to as the "Fee zone".

Overlapping area types Fee zone
Metro/urban and rural Metro/urban
Metro/urban and remote Remote
Rural and remote Remote
How do I pay an NCLL invoice

Payments must be made on the Online Payment and View Invoices webpage.
There are several ways you can reach that page:

  1. you can visit Online Payment and View Invoices directly at any time
  2. on the NCLL portal, you can select the application or licence you wish to pay for and click the "Pay" button in the portal under "Actions"
  3. when you have an invoice due, you will receive an email that contains payment instructions. That email will contain a link to Online Payment and View Invoices
How do I cancel an NCLL invoice?

For a new application, you can cancel the invoice by withdrawing the application. In the NCLL services page, navigate to the NCLL portal, find the application, and under the "Actions" menu select "Withdraw". The application withdrawal will automatically cancel the invoice.

An invoice for a renewable licence can only be cancelled by contacting ISED.

Application process

How long does it take to obtain a licence?

Our service commitment is to provide your NCL licence within 12 weeks.

This commitment is contingent on the availability of the requested spectrum and the completeness of your application, with no errors.

Why might an application be rejected?

An NCL licence application may be rejected if it does not conform to policy, technical or other requirements.

Some examples of reasons for an application being rejected include:

  • the spectrum applied for is not available in the requested area
  • the spectrum aggregation limit would be exceeded

In the 3900 MHz band, the spectrum aggregation limit is exceeded when a licensee has multiple overlapping licences that result in more than 20 MHz of a total bandwidth at any given location. For instance, if the applicant already holds licences for blocks A3 and A4 in a particular area and applies for block A6 in an area that fully or partially overlaps the existing licence area, the application would be rejected.

It's important to note that if the same frequency block is shared between the overlapping licences, it is only counted once when calculating total spectrum usage. Therefore, if both the existing and proposed licences cover the same blocks, the spectrum aggregation limit would not be exceeded.

If an application is rejected, a detailed report will be sent to you via email.

Are licences renewed automatically?

Yes, licences will be automatically renewed if all conditions of licence are met and the renewal invoice is paid in full. The system will send an email to the licensee if it detects an issue that may impact the renewal.

Can I edit a submitted application?

No, it is not possible to edit a submitted application. You can withdraw an application even after you've received an invoice. In the NCLL services page, navigate to the NCLL portal, then find the application, and under the "Actions" menu select "Withdraw".

Can I edit a licence?

No, it is not possible to edit a licence. If you wish to acquire a licence that covers the same area as another licence that you already hold, but with a different technical configuration, then you have two options available to you:

  1. Apply for an overlapping licence while retaining your original licence.
  2. Cancel your original licence by clicking "Cancel licence" under "Actions" in the portal, and apply for a new licence in the same area.

The below table lists the advantages and disadvantages for each option applied to 3900 MHz band.

Option Advantages Disadvantages
1. Apply for an overlapping licence while retaining your original licence Should ISED reject the new licence application, you still retain your original licence Since there is a 20-MHz spectrum aggregation limit, this option only works if both the original licence and the new application are for 10 MHz each
2. Cancel your original licence, and apply for a new licence in the same area You can ask for 20 MHz of spectrum If ISED rejects the new licence application you do not have your original licence to fall back on
What is the maximum licence area permitted in the 3900 MHz band?

The maximum area size per licence is as follows:

  • low power: 15 km²
  • medium power: 165 km²

There is no limit on the number of licences a licensee may hold. For example, to cover an area of 45 km², an applicant could apply for three low power licences with each licence covering 15 km² and must deploy at least one low power base station in each licence area. As a second example, to cover an area of 200 km², an applicant could apply for multiple low power licences or multiple medium power licences or a combination of low power and/or medium power licences, depending on the use case and power requirements.

Can I apply for the full 3900-3980 MHz band?

No. In the 3900 MHz band, ISED has implemented a limit of 20 MHz of spectrum that a licensee can acquire in any given geographic area.

As well, ISED expects that there will be significant demand for this band and that it may take some smaller mobile operators as well as non-traditional users time to develop their business cases for using the spectrum. Given this, ISED is reserving the 3940 MHz to 3980 MHz portion of the 3900 MHz band for small operators including small commercial mobile service providers, non-traditional users, and WISPs.

Equipment

Is there equipment available?

Yes. ISED offers a radio equipment search function to determine availability of equipment. Select advanced search, in the Radio Specification (BETS99-RSS999) box select RSS-198, Flexible Use Broadband Equipment Operating in the Band 3900-3980 MHz (the equipment standard for 3900 MHz), then hit search.

Can I use my existing equipment?

You can use existing equipment so long as it complies with RSS-198,  Flexible Use Broadband Equipment Operating in the Band 3900-3980 MHz, and is certified accordingly. To determine if your equipment is already certified, check the Radio Equipment Search.

If your equipment is not already certified, you would need to complete the steps detailed in RSP-100, Certification of Radio Apparatus and Broadcasting Equipment.

Technical requirements

What is the maximum power and antenna height permitted for an NCLL licence area?

The maximum power and antenna height is determined based on licence type and specific bands of operation. Details on this can be found on our landing page (About NCLL) section or for more details about the 3900-3980 MHz band see SRSP-521, Technical Requirements for Non-Competitive Local Licensed Services, including Fixed and/or Mobile Systems, and Flexible Use Broadband Systems, in the Band 3900-3980 MHz.

Licence areas

What are the rules for the use of 3900-3980 MHz frequencies near the Canada-United States border?

Specific coordination rules (including coordination distances) and procedures for the sharing of the 3900-3980 MHz band between Canadian and United States (US) licensees are under negotiation between ISED and the Federal Communications Commission. Until the international agreements are finalized, NCLL licensees planning to establish or modify a fixed or base station along the Canada-US border shall be required to meet the technical requirements outlined in section 10 of SRSP-521, Technical Requirements for Non-Competitive Local Licensed Services, including Fixed and/or Mobile Systems, and Flexible Use Broadband Systems, in the Band 3900-3980 MHz.

How do I determine if a service area is in a metro, urban, rural, or remote Tier 5 area?

To determine if a service area is in a metro, urban, rural, or remote Tier 5 area, see service areas for competitive licensing. The category breakdown can be viewed by selecting a geographical point on the map or by scrolling through the table.

Are there concerns if my area crosses into another tier area?

There are no concerns if your area crosses into another tier area.

What do the metrics on the portal mean?

The portal presents 4 information summary cards, titled "Applications", "Licences", "Payment due", and "Compliance".

Each of these cards displays a set of metrics. The below table provides a detailed explanation of each displayed metric.

Card Metrics
Applications
  • Draft: The number of applications in the draft state
  • Awaiting approval: The number of applications that have been successfully submitted but not yet authorized/rejected/failed
  • Failed or rejected: The number of applications that have been failed or rejected
  • Submitted applications: The total number of applications that have been submitted (does not include applications that were granted and turned into licences)
Licences
  • Awaiting effective date: The number of active licences that have not yet reached their effective date
  • Active: The number of active licences that have reached their effective date
  • Inactive: The number of expired or revoked licences
  • All licences: The total number of active and inactive licences
Payment due
  • Total balance: The total amount due for the licensee account
  • Applications: The number of applications with unfulfilled invoices awaiting payment
  • Licences: The number of licences with unfulfilled invoices awaiting payment
Compliance
  • Station data overdue: The number of licences that are ineligible for renewal because the licensee has not uploaded site data for them within the last 90 days
  • Deployment required: The number of licences that are ineligible for renewal because it has been two years since the licence's effective data and the licensee has not indicated deployment for them
What do the compliance statuses mean?

You will see a compliance status listed for licences in the portal. There are three possible compliance statuses, detailed in the table below.

Compliance value Description
Blank The licence is either a renewable licence that is currently eligible for renewal, or it is a temporary licence that cannot be renewed.
Station data overdue Station data has not been uploaded for this licence within the past 90 days. If this is a renewable licence, it is no longer eligible for renewal.
Deployment required The licence is ineligible for renewal because it is two years past its effective date and deployment has not been indicated in its station data.
What is the summary report?

If we have rejected your application due to limited spectrum availability, then we will provide you with a summary report that details ISED's spectrum availability analysis. You can download this report by clicking "Download summary report" under "Actions" in the portal.

Licensing process

NCLL is currently available in the following bands:

  • 3900 MHz (3900 to 3980 MHz). This band is comprised of 10 MHz frequency blocks (A1 through A8). In a single application, you can request either one 10 MHz block or two neighbouring (contiguous) blocks up to 20 MHz.
  • 28 GHz (27.50 to 28.35 GHz). This band is comprised of 50 MHz frequency blocks (AA1 through AA17). In a single application, you can request from one to eight neighbouring (contiguous) blocks.

Before you apply, please be sure you understand the obligations of holding an NCL licence.

On this page

  • How to apply
  • Obligations of licensees

How to apply

To apply for an NCL licence, these are the steps to follow.

Step 1: Plan your application

Prepare the information you need for the application form. Determine these key features of an NCL licence:

Frequency band

If you’d like to see which bands and frequency blocks may be available in your desired licence area, please use the Non-Competitive Local Licensing Spectrum Browser.

Amount of bandwidth required and frequency block if applicable

If you want two frequency blocks that aren't contiguous (e.g. A2 and A4), then you must submit two separate applications.

You can request to have a specific frequency block or have us automatically select one for you. Note that large mobile service providers are not eligible to apply for blocks A5-A8 (i.e. 3940-3980 MHz) or blocks AA9-AA17 (i.e. 27.90 to 28.35 GHz).

Licence duration

You have a choice of requesting a renewable or temporary licence.

Renewable: Expires each year on March 31 with the option to renew.

Temporary: Can be from 1 day to 11 months.

You can apply for a licence 90 days in advance of the start date.

Licence area

Identify the location you wish to licence. You can use the NCLL spectrum browser tool on the NCLL services page to draw your area and acquire a list of coordinates in WKT format. Alternatively, the application form also allows you to enter a list of coordinates or to draw your licence area directly on an interactive map.

Step 2: Ensure you have a web profile

If you do not already have one, you can create it by selecting Create your Web Profile – Registration Type. You should receive a response within 5 business days.

Step 3: Fill in the application form

From the NCLL services page, navigate to the NCLL portal then login with your web profile, and select "Start new application". Fill in the information you compiled at step 1 above. Your progress is saved automatically as you proceed, and you can find draft applications in the "Draft applications" section of the portal.

Step 4: Pay your invoice

If your application is approved, then we will send you an invoice, and you will receive your licence once full payment is received.

If your application is not approved, we will keep you informed.

Obligations of licensees

Once your licence is granted, you will be responsible for meeting the conditions of licence. These are band-specific and are printed on the licence. They can also be found here:

  • 3900 MHz: R3 — Spectrum Licences issued via Non-Competitive Local Licensing in the Band 3900-3980 MHz
  • 28 GHz: R5 – Conditions of licence for non-competitive local licences in the 27.5-28.35 GHz band

The list below is shared for your convenience. It contains some of the key requirements common to all NCL licences, including some from other federal government departments and organizations. This isn’t a complete list and it doesn’t replace your due diligence, but it includes some important and often-overlooked items.

Upload your site data information

Within one month of receiving your licence, and continuing on a monthly basis, you will need to provide information about your stations to ISED. Instructions can be found at How to submit a monthly update of technical data.

If you haven’t deployed any stations yet, see instructions at What if I haven’t deployed any stations or am decommissioning stations at the time that my monthly report is due?

If you have previously submitted site data and you haven’t made any changes to your stations since then, see instructions at What if I don’t have any changes to the data that was previously submitted?

Deploy stations

You must deploy at least one station within two years of the effective date of your licence, and start using your network to continuously provide services. This can be for your own internal purposes, or to provide cellular service or wireless Internet service to paying subscribers, or both.

Follow the antenna tower siting procedure

If you are placing or changing any outdoor antennas, you will need to follow the rules provided in CPC-2-0-03 — Radiocommunication and Broadcasting Antenna Systems. This includes the following:

  • Following the rules provided by the appropriate land-use authority
  • Placing antennas on existing structures where possible instead of building new ones (unless the land-use authority requires otherwise)
  • Monitoring and reporting on the amount of radio frequency energy they emit (see Comply with Safety Code 6)

Owners of existing towers are required to allow other organizations to lease space on their towers. For more information see CPC-2-0-17 – Conditions of Licence for Mandatory Roaming and Antenna Tower and Site Sharing and to Prohibit Exclusive Site Arrangements.

Comply with Safety Code 6

Health Canada’s Safety Code 6 guidelines provide limits for safe human exposure to radio frequency energy. Your equipment must comply with Safety Code 6 at all times. Note that it’s not just your own emissions that you have to be aware of: Safety Code 6 requires you to take into account the combined effects of nearby installations within the local radio environment.

For more information see Limits of Human Exposure to Radiofrequency Electromagnetic Energy in the Frequency Range from 3 kHz to 300 GHz - Canada.ca

Comply with cross-border requirements

If your licence area is close to the Canada-U.S. border, you may need to coordinate with operators on the other side of the border in order to reduce the risk of radio interference. Arrangements between ISED and the U.S.’s Federal Communications Commission (FCC) are made on a band-by-band basis, and you will be required to abide by any arrangements that we make.

For the 3900 MHz band, see 3900 MHz NCLL cross-border best practices.

For the 28 GHz band, see the International coordination section in SRSP-522.

Register as a telecommunications provider

If you are offering or providing telecommunications services to the public, you may need to register with the Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission (CRTC).

See Register as a Telecommunications provider to register.

Obtain a Mobile Network Code (MNC)

If you operate equipment that requires an International Mobile Subscription Identity (IMSI) such as LTE or 5G systems, you may qualify for a Canadian-administered Mobile Network Code (MNC) assigned by the Canadian Numbering Administration Consortium (CNAC). A Canadian-administered MNC can be used with Canada's Mobile Country Code (302).

To see if you qualify for a Canadian MNC, see section 7 of the Canadian International Mobile Subscription Identity (IMSI) Assignment Guideline. Note: this is a DOCX document and is in English only.

If you plan to operate a private network and don't qualify for a Canadian-administered MNC, you can consider using Mobile Country Code (MCC) 999, which is a globally shared country code for private networks. With this country code, you can use an MNC of your choice. For more information, see Appendix III of Recommendation ITU-T E.212 (06/2024).
