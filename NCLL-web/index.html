Non-competitive local licensing (NCLL)

Easy access to spectrum

 

Overview

Innovation, Science and Economic Development Canada (ISED) regulates and oversees the regulation and licensing of radio frequency spectrum throughout the country. More information about spectrum, and our role in managing it, can be found at Spectrum in Canada.

In 2024, we introduced a framework known as non-competitive local licensing. This framework is designed to promote the growth of innovative business models, encourage continuous investment in cutting-edge wireless technology like 5G networks, and enhance connectivity in Canada. This framework is also referred to as NCL licensing or as NCLL. NCLL can also stand for "non-competitive local licence" and "non-competitive local licences".

Key qualities

The non-competitive local licensing framework has several key qualities.

  • It is non-competitive. If you apply for a licence and there is no reason for us not to approve the application, then we will approve it, on a first-come first served basis. You do not need to outbid any other company.
  • It is local. The maximum size of an NCL licence is 165 km², and most licences are much smaller. NCLL is designed to be used on a single farm, factory, university campus, or rural community.
  • It is geographically flexible. When you apply for an NCL licence, you tell us where you want it. There is no requirement to stay within a single grid cell or tier area.
  • It can be temporary (from one day up to 11 calendar months) or renewable on an annual basis.
  • It is affordable. Prices vary based on area size, bandwidth, band, and location, but are generally in the range of several hundred dollars a year.
  • The application process is online and not limited to office hours.

Guardrails

NCLL also includes several key guardrails, in order to protect access to spectrum and promote its effective use.

  • Individual bands have band-specific bandwidth limits (spectrum caps), and portions of the band may be set aside for use by smaller and non-commercial operators.
  • When a licence application is submitted, we perform technical analysis in order to predict the effect on existing installations if the licence is granted, as well as their effect on the proposed network.
    • If we determine that there is a significant potential to cause or receive radio interference, we do not grant the application.
    • You can use the NCLL spectrum browser to identify existing installations near your desired licence area.
  • Licensees are not allowed to broadcast beyond the geographical limits of their licence area.
  • Once a licence is granted, the licensee must meet band-specific licence conditions in order to retain their licence. One condition that is common to all NCL licences is that the licensee must build out a network and not simply warehouse the spectrum.
  • Licensees are prohibited from transferring or sharing their licences, even in part.
  • When a licence expires or is cancelled or revoked, the spectrum becomes available once again.

Use cases

Wireless Internet

Deliver wireless Internet services in areas where traditional wired connections are not available or feasible, such as rural or remote locations.

Smart factories

Improve efficiency and productivity by connecting industrial Internet of Things (IoT) devices, enabling real-time monitoring and control of machinery and equipment.

Healthcare facilities

Improve medical device connectivity for real-time monitoring of healthcare devices and support telemedicine services in remote consultations with healthcare professionals.

 
Port and train operations

Optimize cargo handling processes and increase throughput by tracking container, crane automation, and vehicle management.

Educational campuses

Implement campus-wide connectivity, remote learning support, and immersive educational experiences using augmented reality (AR) and virtual reality (VR) technologies.

Smart agriculture

Optimize resources and increase yields by deploying wireless networks for precision farming applications, including crop monitoring, irrigation control, and livestock management.

 
Mining and extraction

Improve productivity and worker safety in challenging environments by establishing wireless networks to enable remote monitoring of equipment, autonomous vehicles, and safety systems.

Fixed wireless access (FWA)

Deliver wireless connectivity to homes and small businesses.

Licensing framework

The NCLL framework is founded in two key documents:

  • Decision on a Non-Competitive Local Licensing Framework, Including Spectrum in the 3900-3980 MHz Band and Portions of the 26, 28 and 38 GHz Bands
  • Addendum to the Non-Competitive Local Licensing Framework to include Spectrum in the 27.5-28.35 GHz Band

The following document provides an in-depth look at NCLL and the practical result of the policy decisions upon which it is founded.

  • CPC-2-1-31 — Licence Application Procedures under the Non-Competitive Local (NCL) Licensing Framework

Restrictions and notes on specific blocks

Certain frequency blocks in each band carry special conditions, as follows:

No interference, no protection

  • 3900 MHz: Block A1
  • 28 GHz: Blocks AA1 and AA2

These blocks may be subject to interference from existing, modified, or new operations in neighbouring bands. NCLL operations in those blocks must not cause interference to those current or future operations, but they will not be protected from them.

Not available to national mobile service providers

  • 3900 MHz: Blocks A5 through A8
  • 28 GHz: Blocks AA9 through AA17

These blocks are not available to national mobile service providers. This applies to any entity with 100,000 or more retail mobile phone subscribers as reported to the CRTC through its Annual Telecommunications Survey. It also applies to affiliates of such an entity, as defined in section 6.2 of Decision on a Non-Competitive Local Licensing Framework, Including Spectrum in the 3900-3980 MHz Band and Portions of the 26, 28 and 38 GHz Bands.

Maximum bandwidth

Each band has a maximum bandwidth that can be held by a licensee in any part of their licence area, as shown in the table above.

If you’re applying for a licence, and part of the licence area overlaps with an NCL licence you already have, then in that overlapping area we will consider all of the bandwidth for both licences. This may result in our rejecting your application, even if the overlap is very small.

However, if the existing licence and the new application share one or more frequency blocks, then those shared blocks are only counted once. As a result, you can have multiple overlapping licence areas in the same location, as long as the total bandwidth used by all of the licences does not exceed the maximum.

As well, we don’t include other spectrum licences, including NCL licences in other bands, in the calculation.

Example

For instance, say you have an existing licence in the 3900 MHz band for frequency blocks A3 and A4, and you’re applying for a second licence that overlaps the first one.

  • If the new licence application is for frequency block A3 and/or A4, then the bandwidth limit will not be exceeded and we will continue to evaluate the application.
  • If the new licence application includes any other frequency block in the 3900 MHz band then the bandwidth limit will be exceeded and we will reject the application.
  • If the new licence application is for a different NCLL band, then the bandwidth limit will not be exceeded and we will continue to evaluate the application.

Non-contiguous blocks

If you require non-contiguous frequency blocks, you may apply for multiple licences with the same licence area, so long as you do not exceed the maximum total bandwidth.

Licence types

NCL licences come in a variety of types: low, medium, and high. Each licence type supports a maximum antenna height and a maximum power level (maximum e.i.r.p.) for fixed and base stations. Details can be found at:

  • 3900 MHz: Section 6.1 of SRSP-521 — Technical Requirements for Non-Competitive Local Licensed Services, including Fixed and/or Mobile Systems, and Flexible Use Broadband Systems, in the Band 3900-3980 MHz
  • 28 GHz: Section 6.1 of SRSP-522 — Technical Requirements for Non-Competitive Local Licensed Systems, including Fixed and/or Mobile Systems, and Flexible Use Broadband Systems, in the Band 27.5-28.35 GHz

Whichever band you choose, if all your stations will be indoors then you must choose a low power licence type. This will allow us to include an attenuation factor that may permit you to obtain a licence area that would otherwise be rejected.

Each licence type comes with a maximum size limit. If you wish to licence an area larger than that limit, you may apply for multiple licences. They can be adjacent and they can even overlap, so long as this does not lead to exceeding the bandwidth limit as noted above.

You can use the NCLL spectrum browser to check whether your intended licence area meets the requirements for your desired band and licence type.

Medium power licences in urban areas in the 3900 MHz band

In the 3900 MHz band, medium power licences are not allowed in urban areas. You can use the NCLL spectrum browser to check your intended licence area.

Fees

The annual licence fee for an NCL licence is calculated by the following equation:

Fee = (Annual base rate) × (total bandwidth in MHz) × (area size in km²)

There is also a minimum annual fee for all NCL licences.

The base rates and minimum annual fee are adjusted annually in accordance with the Consumer Price Index (CPI). For current rates, see Spectrum and telecommunications fees.

Monthly licence fees

For temporary licences, and in the first partial year of renewable licences, we use a monthly fee rather than an annual fee. The monthly base rate and minimum fee are 1/12 of the annual base rate and minimum fee.

  • For temporary licences, the monthly fee is based on the number of calendar months included in the licence. It is calculated according to the rates in place as of the start date, even if the licence continues into the next fiscal year.
  • For renewable licences, the monthly fee is based on the number of calendar months remaining in the current fiscal year for the Government of Canada, which ends March 31. The licence will be eligible for renewal as of April 1 each year thereafter.

Note that the monthly fees are based on calendar months. For example, consider a temporary licence that starts on August 31 and ends on October 1. The fee for this 32-day licence would be the monthly amount times three: August, September, and October.

Licence areas that overlap multiple tier 5 area types

The base rate for an NCL licence is based both on the band and on the tier 5 area type (metro/urban, rural, or remote). Tier 5 area types are displayed in the Non-Competitive Local Licensing Spectrum Browser.

If a licence area overlaps multiple tier 5 area types, the one that is used to determine the base rate is calculated as follows:

Overlapping area types Area type used for base rate
Metro/urban and rural Metro/urban
Metro/urban and remote Remote
Rural and remote

 Remote

Equipment

Each band has rules about the equipment you may use. Details can be found at:

  • 3900 MHz: RSS-198 — Flexible Use Broadband Equipment Operating in the Band 3900-3980 MHz
  • 28 GHz: RSS-193 — Flexible Use Broadband Equipment Operating in the Band 27.5-28.35 GHz

We also provide a Radio Equipment Search tool. To see a complete list of equipment certified for use in your band, use the advanced search feature in that tool and make the following selection in the “Radio Equipment Standard (BETS99-RSS999)” field:

  • 3900 MHz: “RSS198”
  • 28 GHz: “RSS193”

If you would like to use existing equipment that is not on that list, then you can seek to have it certified. The process can be found at RSP-100 — Certification of Radio Apparatus and Broadcasting Equipment. We also have a list of common questions and answers related to the certification process.

How to apply for an NCL licence

Step 1: Ensure you understand all the obligations of holding an NCL licence

See the After your application is approved section below to understand the obligations that apply to all NCL licensees.

Step 2: Plan your application

Prepare the information you need for the application form. The key features of an NCL licence are:

  • Band
  • Specific frequency block if applicable
  • Amount of bandwidth required
  • Start date
  • End date if applicable
  • Licence area (a polygon that you provide, with up to twelve sides)

If you’d like to see which bands and frequency blocks may be available in your desired licence area, please use the Non-Competitive Local Licensing Spectrum Browser.

Step 3: Ensure you have a web account

If you’re not sure whether you have a web account, you can try logging in at Radio Spectrum Management.

If you don’t have a web account, then you can request one at Create your Web Profile – Registration Type. You should receive a response within 5 business days.

Step 4: Fill in the application form

Once you have planned your application and you have a web account, you can log in at Radio Spectrum Management. This will take you to the NCLL portal, where you will find a button marked “Start new application”.

As you proceed through the application, your progress is saved automatically. You can find draft applications in the “Draft applications” section of the portal.

When you reach the final page of the application form and submit the application, we will begin our technical analysis. The process generally takes about a week but can take longer. Our service standard is twelve weeks.

We will send you an email as soon as we have a result. If the result is unsuccessful, we will provide a summary report containing suggestions about what to change if you choose to try again.

After your application is approved

If your application is approved, we will send you an invoice. You can send partial payments, but we will not grant your licence until you pay the invoice in full.

Once your licence is granted, you will be responsible for meeting the conditions of licence. These are band-specific and are printed on the licence. They can also be found here:

  • 3900 MHz: R3 — Spectrum Licences issued via Non-Competitive Local Licensing in the Band 3900-3980 MHz
  • 28 GHz: R5 – Conditions of licence for non-competitive local licences in the 27.5-28.35 GHz band

The list below is shared for your convenience. It contains some of the key requirements common to all NCL licences, including some from other federal government departments and organizations. This isn’t a complete list and it doesn’t replace your due diligence, but it includes some important and often-overlooked items.

Upload your site data (station information)

Within one month of receiving your licence, and continuing on a monthly basis, you will need to provide information about your stations to ISED. Instructions can be found at How to submit a monthly update of technical data.

If you haven’t deployed any stations yet, see instructions at What if I haven’t deployed any stations or am decommissioning stations at the time that my monthly report is due?.

If you have previously submitted site data and you haven’t made any changes to your stations since then, see instructions at What if I don’t have any changes to the data that was previously submitted?

Deploy stations

You must deploy at least one station within two years of the effective date of your licence, and start using your network to continuously provide services. This can be for your own internal purposes, or to provide cellular service or wireless Internet service to paying subscribers, or both.

Follow the antenna tower siting procedure

If you are placing or changing any outdoor antennas, you will need to follow the rules provided in CPC-2-0-03 — Radiocommunication and Broadcasting Antenna Systems. This includes the following:

  • Following the rules provided by the appropriate land-use authority
  • Placing antennas on existing structures where possible instead of building new ones (unless the land-use authority requires otherwise)
  • Monitoring and reporting on the amount of radio frequency energy they emit (see Comply with Safety Code 6)

Owners of existing towers are required to allow other organizations to lease space on their towers. For more information see CPC-2-0-17 – Conditions of Licence for Mandatory Roaming and Antenna Tower and Site Sharing and to Prohibit Exclusive Site Arrangements.

Comply with Safety Code 6

Health Canada’s Safety Code 6 guidelines provide limits for safe human exposure to radio frequency energy. Your equipment must comply with Safety Code 6 at all times. Note that it’s not just your own emissions that you have to be aware of: Safety Code 6 requires you to take into account the combined effects of nearby installations within the local radio environment.

For more information see Limits of Human Exposure to Radiofrequency Electromagnetic Energy in the Frequency Range from 3 kHz to 300 GHz - Canada.ca.

Comply with cross-border requirements

If your licence area is close to the Canada-U.S. border, you may need to coordinate with operators on the other side of the border in order to reduce the risk of radio interference. Arrangements between ISED and the U.S.’s Federal Communications Commission (FCC) are made on a band-by-band basis, and you will be required to abide by any arrangements that we make.

For the 3900 MHz band, see 3900 MHz NCLL cross-border best practices.

A similar list of best practices for the 28 GHz band is forthcoming. Until they are available, please refer to the International coordination section in SRSP-522.

Register as a telecommunications provider

If you are offering or providing telecommunications services to the public, you may need to register with the Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission (CRTC).

See Register as a Telecommunications provider to check if you need to register.

Obtain a Mobile Network Code (MNC)

If you operate equipment that requires an International Mobile Subscription Identity (IMSI) such as LTE or 5G systems, you may qualify for a Canadian-administered Mobile Network Code (MNC) assigned by the Canadian Numbering Administration Consortium (CNAC). A Canadian-administered MNC can be used with Canada’s Mobile Country Code, which is 302.

To see if you qualify for a Canadian MNC, refer to section 7 of the Canadian International Mobile Subscription Identity (IMSI) Assignment Guideline. Note: this is a DOCX document.

If you plan to operate a private network and don’t qualify for a Canadian-administered MNC, you can consider using Mobile Country Code (MCC) 999, which is a globally shared country code for private networks. With this country code, you can use an MNC of your choice. For more information, refer to Appendix III of Recommendation ITU-T E.212 (06/2024). Note: this is a PDF document.

Renewing your licence

If you have a renewable licence and are compliant with the conditions of licence, then we will send you an invoice each year. Once the invoice is paid in full we will automatically renew your licence.

If you have a renewable licence and we detect that you are not compliant with the conditions of licence, then we will let you know so that you can correct the issue.

Notwithstanding the above, we reserve the right to reassign spectrum, and this may mean cancelling renewable licences that are in good standing. However, it is our practice to reassign spectrum only after a fulsome consultation process.

Understanding what you see

Overall layout

The portal includes four panels of information: “Applications”, “Licences”, “Payment due”, and “Compliance”. Here’s what they contain.

  • Draft: The number of applications in the draft state
  • Awaiting approval: The number of applications that have been successfully submitted but not yet authorized, failed or rejected

Licences
  • Awaiting effective date: The number of active licences that have not yet reached their effective date
  • Active: The number of active licences that have reached their effective date
  • Inactive: The number of expired or revoked licences
  • All licences: The total number of active and inactive licences
Payment due
  • Total balance: The total amount due for the licensee account
  • Applications: The number of applications with unfulfilled invoices awaiting payment
  • Licences: The number of licences with unfulfilled invoices awaiting payment
Compliance
  • Station data overdue: The number of licences that are ineligible for renewal because the licensee has not uploaded site data for them within the last 90 days
  • Deployment required: The number of licences that are ineligible for renewal because it has been two years since the licence's effective data and the licensee has not indicated deployment for them

Blank The licence is either a renewable licence that is currently eligible for renewal, or it is a temporary licence that cannot be renewed.
Station data overdue Station data has not been uploaded for this licence within the past 90 days. If this is a renewable licence, it is no longer eligible for renewal.
Deployment required The licence is ineligible for renewal because it is two years past its effective date and deployment has not been indicated in its station data.

Summary reports

If we have rejected your application, then we will provide you with a summary report with an explanation. You can download this report by clicking “Download summary report” under “Actions” in the portal.

Accomplishing tasks

Creating multiple adjacent licence areas

If you wish to provide service to a larger area than is allowed for your licence type, then you can create multiple applications with adjacent licence areas. Steps are as follows:

  1. Create your first licence application. You can either submit it immediately or save it as a draft.
  2. Locate the application in the NCLL portal. From the list of “Actions”, select “Duplicate”. This creates a copy, which will be in draft.
  3. Edit the draft copy, and proceed to the “Licence area” screen.
  4. Edit the licence area to be next to where the original one was.
  5. You can either submit the copy immediately or save it as a draft.
  6. Repeat as necessary.

If you wish to plan out your multiple adjacent licence areas in advance, you can use the Non-Competitive Local Licensing Spectrum Browser. This tool allows you to draw polygons on a map and create WKT representations that you can copy and paste into the NCLL application form.

Editing your submitted application

It is not possible to edit an application once it has been submitted. However, you can withdraw it and submit a new application, even if you’ve received an invoice. Steps are as follows:

  1. Locate the application in the NCLL portal. From the list of “Actions”, select “Withdraw”. This withdraws the application and cancels any associated invoices.
  2. From the list of “Actions”, select “Duplicate”. This creates a copy, which will be in draft.
  3. Edit the draft copy and make any desired changes.
  4. Submit the copy at your convenience.

Paying or cancelling an invoice

You can pay an invoice by visiting Online Payment and View Invoices. This page can be reached directly or via the “Pay” action in the NCLL portal.

If you wish to withdraw an application, you can do that in the NCLL portal as well. This will automatically cancel the related invoice.

If you have received an invoice for a renewable licence and you do not wish to continue operating, please contact ISED at ncll-lncl@ised-isde.gc.ca.

Editing your licence

It is not possible to edit a licence once it has been granted.

If you need to change your technical configuration, you may apply for a new licence. This may be done before or after cancelling your existing licence.

To duplicate your current licence:

  1. Locate the licence in the NCLL portal. From the list of “Actions”, select “Duplicate”. This creates a new licence application, which will be in draft.
  2. Edit the new application and make any desired changes.
  3. Submit the new application at your convenience.

To cancel your current licence:

  1. Locate the licence in the NCLL portal. From the list of “Actions”, select “Cancel licence”.
